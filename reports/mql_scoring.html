How the Model Works

Think of this like a seasoned sales manager who has reviewed thousands of deals and internalized the patterns — who closes, who doesn't, and why. The model learned those patterns automatically from your data.

1

Training on history

We used 2,517 closed deals — 1,368 won, 1,149 lost — as labeled examples. For each deal, we pulled what we knew about the contact at intake time: job title, geography, MQL type, conversion event, TAM enrichment. No post-demo data, no post-deal data.

2

XGBoost (gradient boosting)

The algorithm builds hundreds of decision trees, each one learning from the mistakes of the previous. The result is a model that captures non-linear interactions — e.g., "being a DC from the Midwest who came in through a Webinar" is more predictive than any single factor alone.

3

Scoring today's MQLs

We ran the same feature pipeline over 1,083 contacts with a latest_mql_date and no deal attached. Each contact gets a score 0–100 (their estimated win probability) plus the top 3 factors driving that score up or down.

4

SHAP explains each score

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) tells us exactly why each contact scored the way they did. A positive factor pushes the score up; a negative factor pulls it down. The mql_top_factors column in the table shows the top 3 for every contact.

5

Validation (AUC 0.825)

We validated with 5-fold stratified cross-validation — the model never saw the test fold during training. AUC of 0.825 means when shown a random won deal and a random lost deal, the model ranks the winner higher 82.5% of the time. Baseline random is 50%.

6

How to use the scores

Hot (75+): prioritize immediately — these look like your best historical deals.
Medium (50–74): worth a touch, sequence if not owned.
Low (25–49): nurture, don't spend BDR cycles.
Cold (<25): low fit based on available data — review before outreach.
